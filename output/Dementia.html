Dementia: Overview

  • Dementia is a syndrome characterized by cognitive decline affecting daily functioning.
  • Multiple causes exist, including neurological, neuropsychiatric, and medical conditions.
  • Neurodegenerative dementias (Alzheimer's, Lewy bodies) are prevalent in the elderly, while traumatic brain injury and tumors are common in younger adults.
  • Despite advancements in neuroimaging and biomarkers, disease-modifying therapies are still awaited.
  • Clinicians play a crucial role in alleviating suffering, treating contributing conditions, managing symptoms, promoting brain health, and improving quality of life for patients and families.

Treatment of Dementias

  • Dementia treatment is a neglected area of neurological care for the elderly.
  • Individual interventions may have modest effects, but combined therapies can significantly improve quality of life.
  • Given the widespread impact of dementia, effective therapies hold substantial societal implications.
  • Current treatment focuses on mitigating cognitive and behavioral consequences, as disease-modifying therapies are unavailable.
  • Strategies aim to minimize harm to patients with dementia.

Young-Onset Dementia

  • Young-onset dementia affects individuals under 65 years of age, often misdiagnosed.
  • A systematic approach involving medical history, collateral information, neuropsychological testing, laboratory studies, and neuroimaging aids diagnosis.
  • Differential diagnosis is extensive, including early-onset neurodegenerative conditions (Alzheimer's, vascular dementia, frontotemporal dementia, Lewy bodies, Huntington's, prion disease).
  • Late-onset childhood neurodegenerative conditions (mitochondrial disorders, lysosomal storage disorders, leukodystrophies) may also present as young-onset dementia.
  • Potentially reversible causes (inflammatory disorders, infections, toxic/metabolic abnormalities, transient epileptic amnesia, obstructive sleep apnea, normal pressure hydrocephalus) are crucial considerations.
  • This review provides etiologies, diagnostic strategies, and management options for young-onset dementia.

Young-Onset Dementia: A Heterogeneous Spectrum

  • Young-onset dementia encompasses various dementias with onset before age 65.
  • These include primary dementias (Alzheimer's, frontotemporal, vascular), genetic/familial dementias, metabolic disorders, and secondary dementias (alcohol use disorder, traumatic brain injury, infections).
  • Presentation is diverse, involving cognitive, psychiatric, and neurological symptoms.
  • Diagnostic delay is common, with frequent confusion between young-onset dementia and psychiatric conditions.
  • Thorough assessment is recommended, including collateral history, neuroimaging, lumbar puncture, neuropsychology, and genetic testing.
  • Management requires an age-appropriate, multidisciplinary approach, timely access to services, and family involvement.

Functional Cognitive Disorder: Reframing Cognitive Difficulties

  • Functional cognitive difficulties are increasingly recognized, characterized by internal inconsistency.
  • Impairing or distressing symptoms not explained by other disorders can be conceptualized as functional cognitive disorder (FCD).
  • FCD is likely prevalent but may be underdiagnosed.
  • The term "mild cognitive impairment" (MCI) encompasses various causes, including FCD.
  • A proportion of MCI cases do not progress to dementia, suggesting non-neurodegenerative processes.
  • MCI "non-progressors" lack diagnostic specificity, highlighting limitations of a solely neurodegenerative framework.
  • Depression, anxiety, and behavioral changes can precede neurodegeneration, and further research is needed to explore this relationship in FCD.
  • Viewing MCI as a descriptive term with a broad differential diagnosis, including potentially reversible components like FCD, improves study efficacy and patient outcomes.
  • This paper defines functional neurological disorder-cognitive subtype, explains its position within other cognitive diagnoses and biomarkers, and highlights clinical features for diagnosis.
  • Positive identifiers of FCD improve recognition of individuals not in a neurodegenerative prodrome, facilitating personalized interventions.
