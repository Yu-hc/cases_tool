Involuntary Movements After Stroke

  • Involuntary movements occur in 1-4% of stroke patients.
  • They can occur after ischemic or hemorrhagic strokes affecting the basal ganglia, thalamus, or their connections.
  • Hemichorea-hemiballism is the most common movement disorder after stroke in adults.
  • Dystonia is the most common movement disorder after stroke in children.
  • Other movement disorders include tremor, myoclonus, asterixis, stereotypies, and vascular parkinsonism.
  • Some movements occur immediately after stroke, while others develop later or progressively.
  • Proposed pathophysiological mechanisms include neuronal plasticity, functional diaschisis, and age-related differences in brain metabolism.
  • There are no established guidelines for managing post-stroke movement disorders due to their heterogeneity.

Stroke Prevention: Managing Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Stroke mortality has decreased, but remains preventable.
  • Major modifiable risk factors include hypertension, diabetes mellitus, tobacco smoking, hyperlipidemia, obesity, poor diet/nutrition, and physical inactivity.
  • The article reviews current recommendations for managing each of these risk factors.

Stroke Mimics and Stroke Chameleons

  • Accurate stroke diagnosis is crucial for interventional therapies, which carry potential adverse effects.
  • Stroke mimics are non-cerebrovascular diseases that present with neurological symptoms similar to stroke.
  • Stroke chameleons are strokes with atypical or uncommon symptoms that suggest diseases other than stroke.
  • Detailed history taking, comprehensive neurological examination, and appropriate imaging studies are essential for identification.

Secondary Stroke Prevention: Strategies and Challenges

  • Stroke is a leading cause of disability and a major cause of mortality globally.
  • One in four strokes is recurrent.
  • Secondary stroke prevention starts with understanding the stroke mechanism.
  • Control of vascular risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, smoking cessation) is essential.
  • Lifestyle modifications (healthy diet, aerobic exercise) are recommended.
  • Anticoagulation is the mainstay of therapy for cardioembolism due to atrial fibrillation, mechanical valves, or cardiac thrombus.
  • The role of anticoagulation is less clear for bioprosthetic valves, patent foramen ovale, and dilated cardiomyopathy with low ejection fraction.
  • Surgical intervention for symptomatic extracranial carotid stenosis seems beneficial.
  • Medical therapy for intracranial large artery atherosclerosis includes antiplatelets, high-dose statins, aggressive vascular risk factor control, and lifestyle modifications.
  • Treatment for small artery occlusion (lacunar stroke) is similar to that for intracranial large artery atherosclerosis.
  • Despite advances, stroke recurrence remains high, highlighting the need for novel therapies.
